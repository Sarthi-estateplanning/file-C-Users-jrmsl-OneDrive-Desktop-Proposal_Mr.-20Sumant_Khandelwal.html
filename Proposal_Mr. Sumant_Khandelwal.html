Before We Begin
A Word of Honesty
What Most Families Believe — and Why It Is Not Enough

There is a common belief among families at your stage that a Will alone is sufficient — that writing down who gets what is enough to ensure a smooth transfer of wealth. It is not.

A Will comes into effect only after death. It goes through probate in many cases. It can be challenged. It does not protect assets during your lifetime. It does not address what happens if you are incapacitated rather than gone. And it does not provide the kind of structured, governed distribution that a family of your complexity requires.

The purpose of this engagement is not simply to draft documents. It is to build a structure — one that protects Mrs. Rupa Khandelwal's independence and security, provides a clear and fair inheritance framework for your children and grandchildren, ensures your business interests transition without disruption, and gives you the peace of mind that comes from knowing every question has already been answered.

Part A
The Khandelwal Family Trust
What It Is

The Khandelwal Family Trust is a private, discretionary, irrevocable trust — a standalone legal entity with its own PAN, bank account, and investment accounts, completely separate from your personal estate.

Discretionary means the trustees have the judgment to decide how much each beneficiary receives, when, and for what purpose — based on actual needs at that point in life, not predetermined shares that may not reflect reality years from now.

Irrevocable means once assets enter the trust, they are protected. This is not a limitation — it is the trust's most powerful feature. The assets are working for your family, and they are protected while they work.

What the Trust Is Built For
1
Mrs. Rupa Khandelwal's security and independence

When a patriarch passes, well-meaning relatives from either side often begin to involve themselves in financial decisions that are not theirs to make. The Trust changes this completely. The trustees are defined in the deed. Decision-making authority belongs to them alone. No relative, however senior or well-intentioned, has any legal standing to interfere. Mrs. Rupa Khandelwal, as a trustee, has complete and legally protected control. The Trust is not just a financial instrument — it is her shield.

2
A fair and structured inheritance for your children

Aditya is unmarried today — his life circumstances will change. Your daughters are in their own families, with their own needs and futures. The Trust is designed to provide for each of them fairly, in a structured and purposeful way — not as a lump sum that creates more complexity than clarity. The trustee framework ensures distributions are made thoughtfully, with documented reasoning, not under pressure or in grief.

3
Your grandchildren's future

The Trust can be structured to extend its care to the next generation — ensuring that the wealth you have built continues to serve your grandchildren at appropriate stages of their lives, whether for education, career, health, or long-term welfare.

4
Business continuity and succession

Your business interests require a clear succession pathway. The Trust can hold and govern business assets in a manner that ensures operations are not disrupted and that authority transitions in an orderly, pre-defined manner — not through negotiation at a moment of crisis.

5
Your peace of mind

The Trust, once created and registered, operates according to its deed. You do not need to make fresh decisions every time a need arises. The structure exists, the rules are documented, and the trustees have everything they need to act — for as long as the trust is needed.

How Distributions Work

The Trust deed defines a clear priority framework. Mrs. Rupa Khandelwal's day-to-day needs and long-term security are the first and unrestricted claim on the corpus. Beyond that, distributions to children and grandchildren are guided by trustee judgment, documented purpose, and the values you build into the deed today.

The deed will also define a Negative List — purposes for which the corpus cannot be used — ensuring that your intentions govern the money long after you are no longer around to enforce them personally.

What Can Be Adapted Over Time

The Trust is not a rigid instrument. Distribution amounts, investment approach, operational rules, and trustee composition can all be revisited and adjusted by the trustees as family circumstances evolve. It is designed to remain relevant and purposeful for 20–25 years — not just for the day it is signed.

Part B
The Will

Your Will — and Mrs. Rupa Khandelwal's Will — are the instruments that direct your respective estates at the time of death and ensure your family is provided for exactly as you intend.

A Will is a deeply personal document. What you choose to give, to whom, in what proportion, and at what stage — these are decisions that belong entirely to you. Our role is to translate your intentions into a legally sound, clearly drafted document that leaves no room for ambiguity or dispute.

The Will and the Trust are designed to work in harmony. Which assets pass directly to family members, which are directed into the Trust, and what specific bequests are made — all of this will be discussed and decided with you during the engagement. There is no one right answer. The right answer is the one that reflects your family's needs and your personal wishes.

What each Will will ensure, at a minimum, is that every asset you leave behind has a defined destination, every person you care for is provided for in the way you intended, and nothing is left to assumption or dispute.

The Wills will also include a review of existing nominations across bank accounts, mutual fund folios, insurance policies, and demat accounts. A nomination and a Will can sometimes point in different directions — and where a nomination exists, it typically prevails. Identifying and resolving these misalignments is an important part of ensuring your overall succession plan works as intended.

Part C
Power of Attorney

Most succession planning conversations focus on what happens after death. Very few address what happens if you are alive — but unable to act.

A medical emergency, a period of incapacity, or simply being unavailable at a critical moment can leave your family without the legal authority to manage your bank accounts, business operations, investments, or property matters. In the absence of a properly executed Power of Attorney, even your closest family members have no legal standing to act on your behalf — regardless of your relationship or their intentions.

What It Does

It authorises a trusted person — or persons — to manage your financial, legal, and business affairs on your behalf, to the extent you define. It can be structured as a General POA covering broad authority, or as a Specific POA limited to defined transactions or purposes. We will draft one POA each for Mr. Sumant Khandelwal and Mrs. Rupa Khandelwal, ensuring both are protected and empowered.

Why It Matters at This Stage

At 62, with active business interests and a diversified asset base, the POA is not a document for old age. It is a document for right now — ensuring that if you are ever temporarily unable to act, nothing stalls, no opportunity is lost, and your family is not left navigating institutions and authorities without legal backing.

How It Connects to the Overall Plan

The POA works alongside the Will and the Trust — not as a standalone afterthought. Together, the three instruments ensure that your family is protected and empowered in every scenario: while you are alive and active, while you are alive but unavailable, and after you are gone.
